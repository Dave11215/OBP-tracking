How to run it

1. Same device, every time. Absolute reaction times are NOT comparable to published PVT norms — browsers and touchscreens add a latency offset of roughly 30–100 ms. But that offset is constant on your device, so it cancels out when you compare pre-nap to post-nap. That comparison is the whole point, and it stays valid.

2. Baseline first. Run 7–10 sessions before the pre/post data means anything. The PVT is famously resistant to practice effects, but you still need to know your own day-to-day noise before you can call a change real.

3. Pre-nap: immediately before you lie down.
4. Post-nap: wait 15–20 minutes after waking, so sleep inertia has cleared. Testing at minute one measures grogginess, not restoration.

5. The control condition is not optional. Alertness rises naturally through the morning. If you test at 9am and again at 11am, the clock alone could produce an "improvement." So on some non-nap days, run control-early and control-late at the same two clock times, with no nap between. That isolates the nap from the circadian rise. Without this the whole experiment is confounded.

6. No caffeine between the two tests. Same posture, same room, same brightness.

What the numbers mean

Response speed (mean 1/RT) is the headline metric — it's the most sensitive PVT measure to sleep pressure (Basner & Dinges). Higher is better.
Lapses are the classic sleep-deprivation signature: attention simply drops out. PVT-B counts RT > 355 ms; PVT-10 counts RT > 500 ms. Lower is better.
Slowest 10% degrades before the mean does. Watch it.
False starts are responses before the stimulus (or under 100 ms). A few is normal; many means you're guessing the rhythm.

The prediction on the record

Solance predicted (2026-07-12): post-nap PVT improves measurably — response speed up, lapses down — even though overnight HRV does not move. The HRV null is settled at n=309 days (adjusted effect −0.35 ms, 95% CI −1.29 to +0.58, p=0.46).

CONFIRMS: post-nap improvement clearly exceeds your pre-nap day-to-day variability, AND exceeds the control-early → control-late change on no-nap days.
REFUTES: no improvement beyond noise, or the same improvement shows up on control days without a nap (meaning it was the clock, not the nap).

A refute would be the more interesting result: it would mean the "reboot" is real to you but has no psychomotor correlate at all.
